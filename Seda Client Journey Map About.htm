Seda Client Journey Map

1.   Introduction

Seda engages with a broad range of clients whose situation differs greatly. Each client is hoping to start a business or is already engaged in business and is expecting a unique service from Seda which will in some way add value to their effort

 

Seda therefore, needs to understand the clients situation in such a manner so as to guide the client with the best possible value adding service. This will require an evaluation of the business effort in a concise and informed process to establish the best possible point of entry into the Seda basket of service offerings.

 

Once the client has been serviced, and at regular intervals the same evaluation should be performed to gauge the client progress and growth through the basket of service offerings and business performance.

 

Ultimately, Seda needs to see the client growing and maturing as a business and excelling in business as a result of the assistance and services provided, irrespective of the nature of the business and the stage at which it entered the Seda client database. This iterative evaluation should ideally track a client from ideation, through business Model development, start up, sales traction and eventually to scaling and hopefully exporting.

 

2.   Purpose of Seda Client Journey Map

The purpose of this Map is to provide all practitioners, management and administration staff a guideline as to the stages of businesses Seda encounters and the products and services offered to each phase of business. This will allow all staff to understand what is on offer and what shortcomings exist in the offering. In addition it provides practitioners with a sense of what services are typically required for a business at that particular stage in the growth path. It then provides a distinction between different stages of growth and the requirements for each stage and does away with a one size fits all approach to service delivery.

 

It also draws attention to the needs of business at the various stages and allows administrators to be specific in the development of products for a defined segment of the business continuum.

 

3.   Responsibility

The Seda Client Journey Map is owned by Seda, and is a long standing concept for service delivery at Seda, which has evolved over time. It should be the responsibility of all delivery staff, and those involved in supporting delivery to own the Seda Client Journey Map and to embrace the concepts and apply them as a distinctly and uniquely Seda approach to Enterprise Development Service delivery.

 

 

4.   Operation of the Seda Client Journey Map

The map is divided into 3 zones. These are the:

a.    The concept phase

b.    The pre-commercialisation phase, and

c.    The Commercial phase

Each zone has facilitation events which provide developmental activities for the client. These are depicted by circles and designated as Paths. Each path is specific to the phase and is intended to provide the targeted assistance and growth for the client at that stage in that phase. Each path is designed to move the client to the next stage and phase, to ultimately reach the peak of growth and success.

The perfect and highly realistic scenario would be for a client to seek assistance from Seda when they have no clear business idea. They would enter the ideation Path and search for a problem to solve and a product to match the pain. This would empower the client to find the business concept they seek. Moving from the ideation path to the validation path and validate the product market fit and gain market traction. This would allow the client to refine the business model and identify the specific market where traction can be achieved in the pre-commercialisation phase. Once the business gains tractions and sales begin to materialise emerging in the commercial phase. Graduating to the Organisational development path and building a sustainable business which overcomes all barriers to entry and succeeds in obtaining a highly formalised entity that moves to the Scaling and going Global path. It scales organically and ultimately exports globally and exits Seda as a client by exceeding 200 employees.

 

Unfortunately not all business types will be scalable businesses. There are basically five types of businesses typeswe deal with, and these are:

 

d.   Lifestyle or survivalist Businesses

Typically these are the Music teacher working from home, the sole proprietor Consultant, the lady caterer on the street corner and the Surfer who also teaches surfing classes. Generally Owner operated and employ none or very little additional staff. What level of service should Seda offer such enterprises?

 

e.   Small Businesses

These businesses are the small grocery stores, Bed and Breakfast establishments, Guest Houses, plumbers, electricians, Postnet stores, and other small retail and service businesses. They have no potential to scale and potential for growth in jobs and turnover is capped. In the South African context this also includes Cooperatives. These employ the vast majority of people in the SMME sector, but rarely exceed 20 employees.

Potential for growth is predominantly those small businesses in metropolitan areas where volumes are largest or for opening additional branches in other areas. What are the typical services a Seda branch would offer to such businesses and what are the limitations or exit strategy?

Typically these businesses start with a known product in a known market and the concept and pre-commercialisation phases are short and little validation is required

 

f.    Buyable Businesses

Businesses that are typically easy to start with very little capital requirement, usually in the ICT space and are mobile applications, Internet/online shopping or internet of things and PC Programmes. They are easy to start but generally very difficult to scale as the infrastructure for scaling becomes hugely restrictive and yet have huge potential for scaling. More often they are bought by large companies who themselves have a challenge to scale and need to find new niche products and services of this nature in order to grow their portfolios. How can Seda service such a client?

Often these businesses are targets for corporates to purchase to increase their portfolios of innovation, as they themselves have little or no internal innovative capacity

 

g.    Scalable Businesses

These are the diamonds in the business world as they are born to be big. They have all the potential to scale to global businesses and to create jobs, often in multiple countries, and generate huge returns for the founders and investors. They are often in the Bio-processing, CleanTech, GreenTech, medical device, pharmaceutical and FMCG space. They have a universal problem to solve and innovate to a new level, often with unique intellectual property. Clearly these clients would receive a comprehensive package of Seda services.

These businesses have the potential to create large numbers of jobs in multiple countries and ultimately get listed on a stock exchange and become public companies.

The founders often start multiple companies and often become venture funders

 

h.   Social Enterprise Businesses

Not for profit enterprises are a growing category of start-up businesses, where no product is sold, but a humanitarian service is provided. The HIV/AIDS industry is full of such businesses and the most well know such organisations are Doctors without borders, Habitat for humanity and Gift of the givers. They depend on social awareness grants from donors and provide a service or products free of charge to communities. These can be medical services and often food or temporary housing. Do we offer services to such organisations?

Often they create a huge number of jobs which are time bound and provide skills and experience to many of their employees.

 

Seda should essentially recognise these differences and provide specific support for each, and where necessary limit the support certain categories receive.

 

The following facilitation concepts have been included in the Seda Client Journey Map. These are generic facilitation concepts and are accepted norms across the world in the early stage, start-up enterprises and subsequent growth phases of business development.

 

The Facilitation concepts are:

4.1 Ideation Path
Clients who wish to start a business but who have not thought through a good business idea or who are struggling to develop a sound business idea will be guided through a short process of what constitutes a business idea and the elements that would satisfy a good business idea. Typically there needs to be a problem to solve, a pain point or a job that needs to be performed and solutions to those issues would then constitute a good idea.

 

4.2Business Model Validation Path

When we engage with a client who has a good business idea but is not sure of what the next steps are to getting the business started would typically enter this phase. Here we guide the client to verify that the idea will represent a market opportunity and that there is a market for the solution proposed. There are a few key elements that need to be validated, they are:

i.     The problem exists and people are aware of the problem;

j.     There is a definite solution for the problem in the form of a product or service;

k.    The technology or intellectual property is in the public domain and can be used or the client has developed new technology;

l.     The market is identified and segmented;

m.  The point of entry into the market has been identified and there is potential to dominate the market;

n.    How the business will seek to enter the adjacent markets

o.    The revenue model to be employed in generating an income and covering expenses generated for delivering the product or service and to make a profit.

p.    Ultimately to make the first sales and to gain market traction

 

4.3Organisation development Path

Once a business has market traction and has the potential to grow, the challenges multiply and formalisation, compliance, systems and management become the focus. The list of challenges can be quite long and a number of avenues open up depending on the industry, technology and many other aspects that specific business faces. In this category there should be a large basket of skills, programmes and products to offer the client.

Seda has numerous strategic partners, products and service providers who are able to provide that very specific assistance the client would need. There are also specific paths one could put a client on for a time to achieve a specific developmental goal.

It is an inherent need of the Seda Client Journey Map to be able to service a client in a very specific area of need for a short time with an exit strategy and then to pursue another path altogether to achieve yet another developmental goal or industry requirement.

The Organisation Development path can be further dissected into the following paths:

a.  System Development Path;

b.  Incubation Path;

c.   Productivity Improvement Path.

 

4.4Scaling and Going Global

Scaling a business presents yet another set of challenges for a business. Scaling too fast can lead to failure and scaling too slowly can lead to losing market share due to inability to compete. Going global or exporting is yet another area for development and provides a set of challenges unique to that aspect of business development. These two elements can be very similar or very different and once again will require careful and specific attention different from previous phases of the business

4.5Remediation or Rescue

Not all businesses Seda will engage with are on a growth path. Many a business is in decline and reasons for such can be numerous. These could be due to market share being lost, product life cycle, neglect, poor management and mismanagement. Recognising and developing remedial measures are yet another challenge that Seda client Journey Map must cater for and acknowledge as an area where business development services are required and need to be available.

As far as the evaluation of such a business is concerned the Seda Client Journey Map would need to categorise such a business as needing remediation and once the treatment has been successful the evaluation should categorise the business as back to better ways. So the iterative evaluation is critical in showing the progress of such businesses as well.

5.5Limitations

Although the Seda Client Journey Map seeks to cater for many phases of business it would be unwise to claim that it caters for all eventualities. It does however seek to address the main categories, which is not exhaustive.

This does however, allow for further development of the Seda Client Journey Map when the need arises. It should stimulate the debate and call for practical changes where limitations have been identified which pose a serious material challenge for practitioners.

When interrogating the services offered for each path it might be that for certain categories of clients a reduced offering could be beneficial related to the associated real impact derived

 

 

5.6Entrepreneurship vs Management issues


 

 

Business development has traditionally focused on aspects of business management. It has emerged over the last two or three decades that entrepreneurship and management are not the same disciplines and require different processes and skills.

 

In the Seda Client Journey Map there is a definite distinction made between Entrepreneurial and Management Issues. Clearly these two parts of the Seda Client Journey Map have different elements and will require a different skill set and processes. The Validation process is not a mainstream business development skill and less so the Ideation process or skill.

These may not be common skills and will challenge the uninformed practitioner, manager and administrator. This should not deter from the importance of these elements in the Seda Client Journey Map, but provide an opportunity to explore new processes and to learn new skills, with an open and enquiring mind and approach.

 

In places like Silicon Valley where these modes of thinking originated and have found favour, they are commonplace and expected of start-ups. Furthermore, are seen as basic building blocks of early stage companies and a requirement by most VC and Angel investors there.
